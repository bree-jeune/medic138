## Explanation / Breakdown

### Heat-Related Emergencies

#### Heat Cramps
**What it is:** Painful muscle spasms from electrolyte loss (Na+, K+)

**Presentation:**
- Cramping in legs, arms, abdomen
- Heavy sweating
- Normal mental status
- Normal core temperature

**Management:**
- Rest in cool environment
- Oral rehydration (water + electrolytes)
- Gentle stretching

#### Heat Exhaustion
**What it is:** Volume depletion + electrolyte loss from heat exposure

**Presentation:**
- Heavy sweating (key finding)
- Weakness, fatigue
- Headache, nausea, dizziness
- Core temp: 100-104Â°F
- **Normal mental status** (if altered â†’ heat stroke)

**Management:**
- Remove from heat
- Cool environment
- IV fluids (1-2L NS)
- Cooling measures (wet towels, fan)
- Monitor for progression to heat stroke

#### Heat Stroke
**What it is:** Life-threatening failure of thermoregulation

**Presentation:**
- **Altered mental status** (hallmark)
- Core temp > 104Â°F (often > 106Â°F)
- Hot, DRY skin (classic) OR sweating (exertional)
- Seizures, coma
- Tachycardia, hypotension
- Multi-organ failure

**Two Types:**
- **Classic (non-exertional):** Elderly, chronic illness, medications, hot environment, DRY skin
- **Exertional:** Young, athletic, physical activity, may still be SWEATING

**Management:**
- **COOL IMMEDIATELY** (every minute counts)
  - Remove clothing
  - Cold water immersion (if availableâ€”most effective)
  - Ice packs to groin, axillae, neck
  - Wet sheets + fan
  - **Goal:** Reduce temp to 102Â°F within 30 minutes
- Airway management (often need intubation)
- Large-bore IV access
- Aggressive IV fluids (NS or LR)
- Treat seizures (benzodiazepines)
- Monitor for rhabdomyolysis, DIC, renal failure
- **Rapid transport** to ICU-capable facility

**Critical Pearl:** Heat stroke = altered mental status + elevated temp. If they're confused and hot, it's heat stroke until proven otherwise.

### Cold-Related Emergencies

#### Hypothermia Stages

| Stage | Core Temp | Signs/Symptoms | Treatment |
|-------|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| **Mild** | 32-35Â°C (90-95Â°F) | Shivering, tachycardia, tachypnea, ataxia, impaired judgment | Passive rewarming, warm environment, blankets, warm fluids PO |
| **Moderate** | 28-32Â°C (82-90Â°F) | Shivering stops, bradycardia, bradypnea, confusion, decreased LOC, arrhythmias | Active external rewarming (heat packs, Bair Hugger), warm IV fluids |
| **Severe** | < 28Â°C (< 82Â°F) | Coma, absent reflexes, rigid muscles, VF/asystole, appears dead | Active internal rewarming (warm IV fluids, heated O2, ECMO, bypass) |

**J Wave (Osborn Wave):**
- ECG finding in hypothermia
- Positive deflection at J point (QRS-ST junction)
- Looks like a "hump" after QRS
- Seen in moderate-severe hypothermia

**Management Principles:**
- **Handle gently** - rough handling can trigger VF
- **No CPR if pulse present** (even if very slow)
- **CPR if no pulse** - continue until rewarmed
- **"Not dead until warm and dead"** - continue resuscitation efforts
- Warm fluids (38-42Â°C)
- Cardiac monitoring (expect arrhythmias)
- **No pronouncement until core temp > 32Â°C**

**Rewarming Shock:**
- Hypotension during rewarming
- Caused by:
  - Peripheral vasodilation â†’ decreased SVR
  - Cold, acidotic blood from periphery returning to core
  - Relative hypovolemia
- **Prevention:** Rewarm core before extremities, IV fluids

#### Frostbite

**Classification:**

**Superficial (Frostnip):**
- Skin pale, numb
- No tissue loss
- Rewarms pink

**Superficial Frostbite:**
- Clear blisters
- Skin red/purple after rewarming
- Minimal tissue loss

**Deep Frostbite:**
- Hemorrhagic blisters
- Skin purple/black after rewarming
- Significant tissue loss
- May require amputation

**Field Management:**
- **Do NOT rewarm if risk of refreezing** (causes more damage)
- Remove wet clothing, jewelry
- Protect affected area (sterile dressings, padding)
- **Do NOT rub or massage**
- **Do NOT break blisters**
- Pain management (rewarming is extremely painful)
- If rewarming in field:
  - Use warm water (37-39Â°C / 98-102Â°F)
  - Immerse affected part for 15-30 minutes
  - Until tissue is soft and red
  - Expect severe pain

### High-Altitude Illness

#### Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS)
**What it is:** Mild altitude illness from rapid ascent

**Presentation (above 8,000 ft):**
- Headache (required for diagnosis)
- Nausea, vomiting
- Fatigue, dizziness
- Insomnia

**Management:**
- Stop ascent
- Rest, hydrate
- Acetazolamide (Diamox)
- Descend if worsening
- Oxygen if available

#### High-Altitude Cerebral Edema (HACE)
**What it is:** Severe, life-threatening brain swelling from altitude

**Presentation:**
- Severe headache
- **Ataxia** (can't walk heel-to-toeâ€”diagnostic sign)
- Confusion, altered mental status
- Coma
- Death if untreated

**Management:**
- **IMMEDIATE DESCENT** (only definitive treatment)
- Oxygen (high-flow)
- Dexamethasone 8 mg IV/IM
- Hyperbaric chamber (portable Gamow bag)
- Airway management if obtunded

#### High-Altitude Pulmonary Edema (HAPE)
**What it is:** Fluid accumulation in lungs from altitude

**Presentation:**
- Dyspnea at rest
- Cough (dry â†’ productive, frothy)
- Crackles, wheezing
- Cyanosis
- Tachycardia, tachypnea

**Management:**
- **IMMEDIATE DESCENT**
- Oxygen (high-flow, keep SpO2 > 90%)
- Nifedipine 30 mg extended-release PO
- Hyperbaric chamber
- Keep warm (cold worsens)
- CPAP if available

**Critical Pearl:** HACE and HAPE are FATAL without descent. Medications buy time, but getting down saves lives.

### Submersion Injuries (Drowning)

**Drowning = respiratory impairment from submersion in liquid**

**Classification:**
- **Non-fatal drowning:** Survived (with or without injuries)
- **Fatal drowning:** Death from drowning

**Pathophysiology:**
- Breath-holding â†’ hypoxia
- Aspiration (or laryngospasm)
- Hypoxic brain injury
- Hypothermia (if cold water)

**Presentation:**
- Coughing, dyspnea
- Hypoxia, cyanosis
- Altered mental status
- Pulmonary edema
- Cardiac arrest

**Management:**
- **C-spine precautions if diving/trauma suspected**
- Airway management
  - Suction
  - High-flow O2
  - CPAP/BiPAP if conscious
  - Intubation if severe
- Cardiac monitoring
- Rewarm if hypothermic
- **All drowning victims need hospital evaluation** (delayed pulmonary edema)

**Cold Water Drowning:**
- Hypothermia protective (slows metabolism)
- Prolonged submersion with survival possible
- Continue resuscitation until rewarmed

**Do NOT attempt abdominal thrusts or "drain water"** - it doesn't work and delays CPR.

### Lightning and Electrical Injuries

**Lightning Strike Pathophysiology:**
- Massive DC current (30,000-50,000 amps)
- Duration: milliseconds
- Can cause:
  - Cardiac arrest (asystole more common than VF)
  - Respiratory arrest (prolonged apnea)
  - Neurological injury
  - Burns (superficial and deep)
  - Trauma (blast effect)

**Presentation:**
- Cardiac arrest
- Confusion, amnesia, paralysis
- Tympanic membrane rupture
- Burns (Lichtenberg figuresâ€”fernlike patterns)
- Blunt trauma from being thrown

**Management:**
- **Reverse triage** (treat "dead" firstâ€”they may recover with CPR)
- CPR if arrested
- Cardiac monitoring (arrhythmias common)
- Cervical spine precautions
- Treat burns
- Monitor for rhabdomyolysis, compartment syndrome

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake #1: Waiting to Cool Heat Stroke
Every minute of hyperthermia = more brain damage. Cool first, transport second.

### Mistake #2: Aggressive Rewarming in Severe Hypothermia
Rewarming too fast causes rewarming shock. Core before periphery.

### Mistake #3: Rewarming Frostbite When Refreezing Is Possible
Thaw-refreeze-thaw causes worse tissue damage. Protect and transport.

### Mistake #4: Assuming All Victims at a Lightning Strike Are Dead
"Reverse triage" - those who appear dead may recover. Prioritize them.

### Mistake #5: Missing Delayed Pulmonary Edema After Drowning
All drowning victims need hospital evaluation, even if they seem fine.

### Mistake #6: Giving Up CPR Too Soon in Cold Water Drowning
Hypothermia is protective. "Not dead until warm and dead."

**Answer:** Severe hypothermia with pulse - gentle handling, active rewarming, no CPR

**Why:**

- J waves on ECG confirm hypothermia
- Rigid, appears dead (but isn't)

6. **Active rewarming:**
   - Heat packs to trunk (NOT extremities)

8. IV access
9. Check glucose (hypoglycemia common in hypothermia)

11. Continue rewarming during transport

**Do NOT:**
- Rewarm extremities first (causes rewarming shock)
- Give cold IV fluids
- Handle roughly
- Assume patient is dead

**Prognosis depends on rewarming success. Many survive with aggressive rewarming.**

### Case 3: The Mountain Climber

**Patient:** 35-year-old male, climbing at 14,000 ft, severe headache, vomiting, can't walk straight  
**Exam:** Ataxic gait (can't do heel-to-toe), confused, SpO2 82%  
**Friend:** "We came up too fast. He's been sick for 12 hours."

**What's the diagnosis and treatment?**

<details>
<summary>Click for answer</summary>

**Answer:** High-Altitude Cerebral Edema (HACE) - immediate descent required

**Why:**
- Altitude > 8,000 ft
- Severe headache + ataxia = HACE (diagnostic)
- Confusion (worsening)
- This is LIFE-THREATENING

**Action:**
1. **IMMEDIATE DESCENT** (only definitive treatment - DO NOT DELAY)
2. High-flow oxygen (keep SpO2 > 90%)
3. Dexamethasone 8 mg IV/IM (or 4 mg PO if no IV)
4. Hyperbaric chamber (Gamow bag) if available (temporizing, not definitive)
5. Airway management if worsening LOC
6. Keep warm
7. Descend as low as possible (even 1,000-2,000 ft helps)

**Do NOT:**
- Continue ascent or stay at altitude
- Assume it will resolve on its own
- Delay descent to "see if meds work"

**HACE is fatal without descent. Every hour at altitude = more brain damage.**

</details>

**Patient:** 6-year-old boy, pulled from pool after ~3 minutes, bystander CPR started  
**Exam:** Unconscious, agonal respirations, weak pulse 50 bpm, cold to touch  
**Temp:** 32Â°C (89.6Â°F)

**What's your management?**

<details>
<summary>Click for answer</summary>

**Answer:** Cold water drowning with pulse - aggressive airway management + rewarming

**Why:**
- Submersion injury
- Hypothermia (cold water protective)
- Pulse present (even if weak)
- Agonal breathing (inadequate ventilation)

**Action:**
1. **C-spine precautions** (diving/trauma can't be ruled out)
2. **Aggressive airway management:**
   - Suction (if needed)

3. Cardiac monitoring
4. IV/IO access
5. **Rewarming:**
   - Remove wet clothing
   - Dry patient

   - Warm IV fluids (if giving)

7. Monitor for:
   - Pulmonary edema (delayed)
   - Arrhythmias
   - Neurological injury

- Attempt abdominal thrusts to "drain water"
- Give up resuscitation early (hypothermia is protective)
- Skip hospital transport even if patient improves

### Case 5: The Lightning Strike

**Scene:** Multiple people struck by lightning during storm  
**Victim 1:** Conscious, confused, burns to leg  
**Victim 2:** Unconscious, no pulse, no respirations  
**Victim 3:** Conscious, complaining of ear pain and vision changes

**Who do you treat first?**

<details>
<summary>Click for answer</summary>

**Answer:** Victim 2 (the "dead" one) - REVERSE TRIAGE in lightning strikes

**Why:**
- Lightning causes asystole/prolonged apnea
- Victim 2 may recover with CPR (respiratory arrest likely)
- Victims 1 and 3 are conscious â†’ stable for now
- **Reverse triage:** Treat the dead first (opposite of mass casualty triage)

**Action for Victim 2:**
1. **Begin CPR immediately**
2. AED/cardiac monitor
3. Airway management
4. IV access
5. C-spine precautions (blast effect)
6. Continue resuscitation (better prognosis than typical cardiac arrest)

**Action for Victim 1:**
- C-spine precautions
- Cardiac monitoring (arrhythmias common)
- Treat burns
- Neurological assessment
- Monitor for rhabdomyolysis

**Action for Victim 3:**
- Assess for TM rupture (ear pain)
- Visual assessment (corneal injury, cataracts)
- Neurological exam
- Cardiac monitoring

**All lightning victims need transport and monitoring (delayed complications common).**

</details>
