## Explanation / Breakdown

### Burn Depth Classification

#### Superficial (First-Degree)
- **Damage:** Epidermis only
- **Appearance:** Red, painful, dry, no blisters
- **Example:** Mild sunburn
- **Heals:** 3-7 days, no scarring
- **Treatment:** Minor, doesn't count toward TBSA for fluid resuscitation

#### Partial-Thickness (Second-Degree)
**Superficial Partial:**
- **Damage:** Epidermis + upper dermis
- **Appearance:** Red, very painful, blisters, moist
- **Sensation:** Intact (extremely painful)
- **Heals:** 2-3 weeks, minimal scarring

**Deep Partial:**
- **Damage:** Epidermis + deep dermis
- **Appearance:** White/yellow, less painful, may blister
- **Sensation:** Decreased
- **Heals:** 3-8 weeks, significant scarring

#### Full-Thickness (Third-Degree)
- **Damage:** Entire dermis + subcutaneous tissue
- **Appearance:** White, brown, leathery, dry, charred
- **Sensation:** **Painless** (nerve endings destroyed)
- **Heals:** Does NOT heal on its own, needs grafting
- **Scarring:** Severe, contractures likely

#### Fourth-Degree
- **Damage:** Through skin into muscle, tendon, bone
- **Appearance:** Charred, black
- **Outcome:** Often requires amputation

### Total Body Surface Area (TBSA) Estimation

#### Rule of Nines (Adult)

| Body Region | Percentage |
|-------------|------------|
| Head & Neck | 9% |
| Each Arm | 9% (front and back) |
| Each Leg | 18% (front and back) |
| Anterior Trunk | 18% |
| Posterior Trunk | 18% |
| Perineum | 1% |

**Total = 100%**

#### Rule of Nines (Pediatric - Modified)

| Body Region | Infant/Child % |
|-------------|----------------|
| Head & Neck | 18% (larger head) |
| Each Arm | 9% |
| Each Leg | 14% (smaller legs) |
| Anterior Trunk | 18% |
| Posterior Trunk | 18% |
| Perineum | 1% |

#### Palm Method
- Patient's **palm = 1% TBSA**
- Useful for scattered burns
- Fingers NOT included (just the palm)

### Burn Center Criteria (ABA Guidelines)

Transfer to a burn center if:

1. **Partial-thickness > 10% TBSA**
2. **Full-thickness burns (any size)**
3. **Burns to face, hands, feet, genitals, perineum, major joints**
4. **Chemical burns**
5. **Electrical burns (including lightning)**
6. **Inhalation injury**
7. **Circumferential burns (extremity or chest)**
8. **Burns + trauma (burn injury poses greatest risk)**
9. **Burns in patients with significant comorbidities**
10. **Pediatric burns (if facility lacks pediatric expertise)**

### Fluid Resuscitation: Parkland Formula

**For burns > 20% TBSA in adults (>10% in children):**

**Parkland Formula:**  
**4 mL x weight (kg) x % TBSA = Total fluid in 24 hours**

**First 8 hours:** Give 50% of total  
**Next 16 hours:** Give remaining 50%

**Use Lactated Ringer's (LR) - NOT normal saline**

**Example:**  
70 kg patient, 30% TBSA burn  
4 x 70 x 30 = 8,400 mL in 24 hours  
First 8 hours: 4,200 mL (525 mL/hour)  
Next 16 hours: 4,200 mL (262 mL/hour)

**Start time = time of injury, not time of arrival**

### Inhalation Injury

**Suspect inhalation injury if:**
- Fire in enclosed space
- Facial burns
- Singed nasal hairs or eyebrows
- Carbonaceous sputum (black/sooty)
- Hoarseness, stridor, wheezing
- Respiratory distress
- Altered mental status (CO poisoning)

**Why it matters:**  
Inhalation injury increases mortality by 20%. Airway swelling can be rapid and catastrophic.

**Management:**
- High-flow O2 (100% NRB or BVM)
- Early intubation if any concern (don't wait for stridor)
- Large ETT (7.5-8.0) - swelling makes reintubation impossible
- Consider CO poisoning (check COHgb if able)

### Special Burn Types

#### Electrical Burns
- **Danger:** Internal damage far exceeds external burns
- **Complications:** Cardiac arrhythmias, rhabdomyolysis, compartment syndrome
- **Management:** Cardiac monitoring, aggressive fluids, check CK levels

#### Chemical Burns
- **Management:** Brush off dry powder FIRST, then irrigate copiously (20-30 min minimum)
- **Exception:** Elemental sodium/potassium (reacts with water - use oil-based coverage)
- **Don't neutralize** - causes exothermic reaction (more damage)

#### Circumferential Burns
- **Danger:** Acts like a tourniquet â†’ compartment syndrome
- **Chest:** Restricts breathing â†’ escharotomy needed
- **Extremity:** Cuts off circulation â†’ escharotomy needed
- **Signs:** Decreased pulses, capillary refill, sensation, movement

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake #1: Including First-Degree Burns in TBSA
Superficial burns don't count toward fluid resuscitation calculations. Only partial and full-thickness.

### Mistake #2: Applying Ice or Cold Water
Causes hypothermia and worsens tissue damage. Use room-temperature water or saline-soaked dressings.

### Mistake #3: Delaying Intubation in Inhalation Injury
Airway swelling happens FAST. If you think they need a tube, they need it NOW.

### Mistake #4: Popping Blisters
Leave them intact. They're sterile and protective. Popping increases infection risk.

### Mistake #5: Under-Resuscitating
Burns need MASSIVE fluid volumes. Don't be shy with the LR.

### Mistake #6: Using Normal Saline Instead of LR
Parkland formula calls for Lactated Ringer's. NS can cause hyperchloremic acidosis.

### Mistake #7: Forgetting Pain Management
Burns are excruciating. Give opioids early and often (IV, titrated to effect).

## Practice Cases

### Case 1: The Kitchen Fire

**Patient:** 32-year-old female, grease fire, burns to both arms (circumferential) and anterior chest  
**Exam:** Blisters, red, moist, extremely painful, crying  
**Vitals:** BP 142/88, HR 118, RR 22, SpO2 98% RA

**What's the TBSA and what's your treatment plan?**

<details>
<summary>Click for answer</summary>

**Answer:** 27% TBSA, partial-thickness burns, needs burn center

**TBSA Calculation:**
- Both arms: 9% + 9% = 18%
- Anterior chest: 9% (half of anterior trunk)
- **Total: 27%**

**Treatment Plan:**
- **Burn center criteria met** (>10% partial-thickness + circumferential)
- **IV access x2** (large-bore, 18G or larger)
- **Fluid resuscitation (Parkland):** 
  - Assume 60 kg = 4 x 60 x 27 = 6,480 mL over 24 hours
  - First 8 hours: 3,240 mL (405 mL/hr)
- **Pain management:** Morphine 2-4 mg IV, titrate
- **Remove jewelry immediately** (swelling will worsen)
- **Cover with dry sterile dressings**
- **Keep warm**
- **Continuous monitoring**
- **Transport to burn center**

### Case 2: The Enclosed Space Fire

**Patient:** 58-year-old male, pulled from burning bedroom, confused, coughing black sputum  
**Exam:** Burns to face, singed eyebrows/nasal hairs, hoarse voice  
**Vitals:** BP 158/92, HR 128, RR 28, SpO2 94% on NRB

**What's your biggest concern?**

<details>
<summary>Click for answer</summary>

**Answer:** Inhalation injury with impending airway compromise

**Red Flags:**
- Enclosed space fire (smoke inhalation)
- Facial burns
- Singed hairs
- Carbonaceous sputum
- Hoarseness (laryngeal edema beginning)
- Confusion (possible CO poisoning)
- Increased respiratory rate

**Action:**
- **Intubate NOW** - Don't wait for stridor (that's a late sign)
- Use large ETT (7.5-8.0)
- 100% O2
- Aggressive fluid resuscitation (facial burns + possible CO)
- Consider CO poisoning (check COHgb if available)
- Burn center with hyperbaric O2 capability if COHgb >25%
- Continuous cardiac monitoring

**This patient will deteriorate rapidly. Early intubation saves lives.**

</details>

### Case 3: The Electrical Injury

**Patient:** 23-year-old male, contacted power line, entrance wound on right hand, exit wound on right foot  
**Exam:** Small charred areas on hand/foot, otherwise appears stable  
**Vitals:** BP 118/76, HR 102 (irregular), RR 16, SpO2 99% RA

**What are your concerns beyond the visible burns?**

<details>
<summary>Click for answer</summary>

**Answer:** Internal tissue damage, cardiac arrhythmias, rhabdomyolysis, compartment syndrome

**Why:**
- Electrical current travels through body (not just surface)
- Damages muscle, nerves, vessels internally
- Entrance/exit wounds are just "tip of iceberg"
- High voltage = high risk

**Concerns:**
- **Cardiac:** Arrhythmias (note irregular HR), cardiac arrest
- **Muscle:** Rhabdomyolysis â†’ myoglobin â†’ renal failure
- **Compartment syndrome:** Swelling in fascial compartments
- **Neurological:** Spinal cord injury, peripheral nerve damage

**Action:**
- **Continuous cardiac monitoring** (watch for VF, VT, heart blocks)
- **Large-bore IV access**
- **Aggressive fluid resuscitation** (even with small surface burns)
  - Goal: Urine output 1-2 mL/kg/hr (higher than typical burns)
  - Dark urine = myoglobinuria
- **Check distal pulses, sensation, motor function** (compartment syndrome)
- **Transport to burn center** (all electrical burns qualify)
- **Labs:** CK, troponin, myoglobin, renal function

**Don't be fooled by small external burns. Electrical injuries kill from the inside.**

</details>

### Case 4: The Chemical Splash

**Patient:** 40-year-old industrial worker, sulfuric acid splashed on chest and abdomen  
**Scene:** Co-workers rinsed with water for ~2 minutes  
**Exam:** Red, painful, skin sloughing in areas

**What's your immediate action?**

<details>
<summary>Click for answer</summary>

**Answer:** Continue irrigation, remove contaminated clothing, monitor for systemic effects

**Action:**
- **Continue irrigation** - 20-30 minutes minimum
  - Use copious amounts of water or saline
  - Brush off any dry chemical first (if powder)
- **Remove all clothing** (contaminated)
- **Protect yourself** - PPE, don't touch contaminated areas
- **Do NOT neutralize** - causes heat, more damage
- **Monitor airway** - chemical fumes can cause inhalation injury
- **Pain management** - chemical burns are extremely painful
- **IV access + fluids**
- **Transport to burn center** (all chemical burns qualify)

**Special Considerations:**
- Sulfuric acid = exothermic reaction (generates heat)
- Delayed tissue damage can occur (looks minor initially, worsens over hours)
- Check MSDS/hazmat info if available
- Consider systemic absorption (some chemicals absorbed through skin)

</details>

### Case 5: The Pediatric Burn

**Patient:** 3-year-old pulled pot of boiling water off stove, burns to face, anterior chest, and both arms  
**Exam:** Screaming, red/blistered skin, moist  
**Vitals:** HR 165, RR 38, crying

**What's the TBSA using pediatric Rule of Nines?**

<details>
<summary>Click for answer</summary>

**Answer:** 36% TBSA - this child is critically burned

**Pediatric TBSA Calculation:**
- Face: 9% (half of head/neck = 18%)
- Anterior chest: 18%
- Both arms: 9% + 9% = 18%
- **Total: 45%**

Wait - let's recalculate. If face only (not full head):
- Face: ~4.5% (quarter of head)
- Anterior chest: 18%
- Both arms: 9% + 9% = 18%
- **Total: 40.5%**

**Critical Points:**
- **>10% TBSA in child = burn center**
- **This child is in shock** (HR 165 is too high even for age/pain)
- **Massive fluid needs**

**Action:**
- **IO access if IV difficult** (don't delay)
- **Fluid resuscitation (Parkland):**
  - Estimate weight: 3 years old ~15 kg
  - 4 x 15 x 40 = 2,400 mL over 24 hours
  - First 8 hours: 1,200 mL (150 mL/hr)
- **Pain management:** Morphine 0.1 mg/kg IV/IO
- **Keep warm** (kids lose heat fast)
- **Calm child + parent** (if parent not injured)
- **Immediate transport to pediatric burn center**
- **Notify receiving facility early** (they need to mobilize resources)

**Outcome depends on speed of resuscitation and specialized care.**

</details>
