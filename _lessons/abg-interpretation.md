## Arrival Scenario

You're transporting a 68-year-old COPD patient from a community hospital. He's on BiPAP at 12/5, FiO2 50%, with decreased level of consciousness.

The ABG drawn 30 minutes ago:
- **pH:** 7.28
- **PaCO2:** 68 mmHg
- **PaO2:** 58 mmHg
- **HCO3:** 31 mEq/L

The nurse says "it's compensated respiratory acidosis."

**Your first thought should be:** Is this actually compensated? What does this mean for my transport plan?

- **pH and CO2 opposite** â†’ Respiratory problem

### Step 3: Check Compensation

- **Respiratory problems** â†’ kidneys compensate (24-48 hours)

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake #1: Calling It "Compensated" When pH Is Abnormal
If pH is outside 7.35â€“7.45, it's NOT fully compensated.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Oxygenation
Perfect acid-base but PaO2 < 60? Still a problem.

### Mistake #3: Forgetting Mixed Disorders
Sometimes both respiratory AND metabolic problems exist.

### Mistake #4: Over-Trusting "Normal" pH
pH 7.38 with abnormal CO2 and HCO3 = full compensation (still significant).
