## What Is Cardiac Axis?

The **cardiac axis** is the average direction of electrical activity during ventricular depolarization.

Normal is roughly "down and to the left" (toward the apex).

**Why it matters:**
- Suggests structural changes, conduction blocks, or chamber enlargement
- Helps differentiate certain arrhythmias
- Can reveal misplaced leads

## Why Does Axis Deviate?

**Left Axis Deviation (LAD):**
- Left ventricular hypertrophy
- Left anterior fascicular block (LAFB)
- Inferior MI (old)
- Left bundle branch block

**Right Axis Deviation (RAD):**
- Right ventricular hypertrophy
- Pulmonary embolism (acute)
- Left posterior fascicular block
- Normal in tall, thin people

**Extreme RAD:**
- Lead misplacement (check first!)
- Ventricular tachycardia
- Severe RVH
- Hyperkalemia

---

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake #1: Ignoring Axis Altogether
Axis gives you clues about underlying pathology. Don't skip it.

### Mistake #2: Confusing LAD with LBBB
LBBB causes a wide QRS. LAD just changes direction.

### Mistake #3: Assuming RAD = PE
RAD can suggest PE, but needs clinical context.

### Mistake #4: Not Checking for Lead Misplacement
Extreme RAD? Check your stickers. Arm reversal causes bizarre axis.
