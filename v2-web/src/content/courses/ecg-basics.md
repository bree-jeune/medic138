## Explanation / Breakdown

### ECG Paper Basics

**Standard paper speed:** 25 mm/sec

**Horizontal axis = TIME**
- **Small box:** 0.04 seconds (40 milliseconds)
- **Large box:** 0.20 seconds (200 milliseconds)
- **5 large boxes:** 1 second
- **300 large boxes:** 1 minute

**Vertical axis = VOLTAGE**
- **Small box:** 0.1 mV (1 mm)
- **Large box:** 0.5 mV (5 mm)

### The Cardiac Cycle on ECG

**P Wave**
- **Represents:** Atrial depolarization
- **Duration:** < 0.12 seconds (< 3 small boxes)
- **Amplitude:** < 2.5 mm in limb leads
- **Tells you:** SA node fired, atria are contracting

**PR Interval**
- **Represents:** Time from atrial depolarization to ventricular depolarization
- **Measured:** Start of P wave to start of QRS
- **Normal:** 0.12 - 0.20 seconds (3-5 small boxes)
- **Tells you:** AV node conduction time

**QRS Complex**
- **Represents:** Ventricular depolarization
- **Duration:** < 0.12 seconds (< 3 small boxes)
- **Q wave:** First downward deflection
- **R wave:** First upward deflection
- **S wave:** Downward deflection after R wave
- **Tells you:** Ventricles are contracting

**ST Segment**
- **Represents:** Early ventricular repolarization
- **Measured:** End of QRS to start of T wave
- **Should be:** Isoelectric (flat, at baseline)
- **Tells you:** If elevated or depressed â†’ ischemia/infarction

**T Wave**
- **Represents:** Ventricular repolarization
- **Shape:** Rounded, asymmetric
- **Direction:** Usually same as QRS
- **Tells you:** If inverted/peaked â†’ ischemia, electrolyte abnormality

**QT Interval**
- **Represents:** Total time for ventricular depolarization + repolarization
- **Measured:** Start of QRS to end of T wave
- **Normal:** < 440 ms (males), < 460 ms (females)
- **Corrected for heart rate:** QTc (use Bazett formula)
- **Tells you:** If prolonged â†’ risk of torsades de pointes

| V1-V2 (tall R) | Posterior | RCA or LCX |

### Systematic ECG Interpretation

Use this every single time:

**1. Rate**
- **Method 1 (Regular rhythm):** 300 / (# large boxes between R waves)
- **Method 2 (Irregular rhythm):** Count R waves in 6 seconds x 10
- **Method 3 (Very fast):** 1500 / (# small boxes between R waves)

**2. Rhythm**
- Regular or irregular?
- Are P waves present?
- Is there a P before every QRS?
- Is there a QRS after every P?

**3. Axis**
- Use Lead I and aVF (see Axis Deviation course)

**4. Intervals**
- **PR interval:** Normal (0.12-0.20), short, or prolonged?
- **QRS duration:** Narrow (< 0.12) or wide (â‰¥ 0.12)?
- **QT interval:** Normal or prolonged?

**5. Morphology**
- Hypertrophy (high voltage)?
- Bundle branch blocks (wide QRS with specific patterns)?
- Q waves (old MI)?

**6. ST Segments & T Waves**
- ST elevation (STEMI)?
- ST depression (ischemia)?
- T wave inversions (ischemia, old MI)?
- Hyperacute T waves (early STEMI)?

### Common Rhythms

**Normal Sinus Rhythm**
- Rate: 60-100 bpm
- Regular rhythm
- P before every QRS
- QRS after every P
- PR interval normal

**Sinus Bradycardia**
- Rate: < 60 bpm
- Otherwise normal sinus rhythm
- Causes: Athletes, meds (beta blockers), vagal tone, hypothermia, increased ICP

**Sinus Tachycardia**
- Rate: > 100 bpm
- Otherwise normal sinus rhythm
- Causes: Pain, fever, anxiety, hypovolemia, shock, PE, drugs

**Atrial Fibrillation**
- No P waves
- Irregularly irregular rhythm
- Narrow QRS (usually)
- Undulating baseline (fibrillatory waves)

**Atrial Flutter**
- "Sawtooth" flutter waves (best in II, III, aVF)
- Regular or irregular rhythm
- Atrial rate ~300 bpm
- Ventricular rate depends on AV conduction (2:1, 3:1, 4:1 block)

**SVT (Supraventricular Tachycardia)**
- Rate: 150-250 bpm
- Regular rhythm
- Narrow QRS
- P waves often hidden in QRS or T wave

**Ventricular Tachycardia**
- Rate: > 100 bpm (usually 120-250)
- Regular rhythm (usually)
- Wide QRS (â‰¥ 0.12 seconds)
- No clear P waves

**Ventricular Fibrillation**
- Chaotic, disorganized electrical activity
- No identifiable waves
- Fatal without immediate defibrillation

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake #1: Not Using a Systematic Approach
Skipping steps leads to missed findings. Use the same approach every time.

### Mistake #2: Calling Everything "Normal Sinus Rhythm"
Look closer. There's always something to find or rule out.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring the Clinical Picture
ECG interpretation without context is worthless. Know your patient.

### Mistake #4: Misplacing Leads
V1-V6 in the wrong spots changes the entire interpretation. Know your landmarks.

### Mistake #5: Missing Subtle ST Changes
Not all STEMIs are dramatic. Look for 1 mm elevation in 2 contiguous leads.

### Mistake #6: Assuming Wide Complex = VT
Could also be SVT with aberrancy, bundle branch block, or WPW. Context matters.

## Practice Cases

### Case 1: The Subtle STEMI

**Patient:** 58-year-old male, "indigestion" x 30 minutes  
**Vitals:** BP 138/82, HR 88, RR 18  
**ECG:** Regular rhythm, rate 88, ST elevation 1.5 mm in leads III and aVF, ST depression 1 mm in leads I and aVL

**What's the diagnosis?**

<details>
<summary>Click for answer</summary>

**Answer:** Inferior STEMI

**Why:**
- ST elevation â‰¥1 mm in 2 contiguous leads (III, aVF = inferior)
- Reciprocal ST depression in I, aVL (confirms real STEMI, not artifact)
- Likely RCA occlusion

**Action:**
- Activate cath lab
- Aspirin 324 mg PO
- Nitro 0.4 mg SL (if BP allows)
- IV access
- Get right-sided ECG (V3R, V4R) - rule out RV involvement
- Serial 12-leads
- Rapid transport

**Clinical Pearl:** Reciprocal changes = real STEMI. This isn't artifact or early repolarization.

</details>

### Case 2: The Fast Heart

**Patient:** 34-year-old female, palpitations x 20 minutes, lightheaded  
**Vitals:** BP 102/64, HR 180, RR 22  
**ECG:** Regular rhythm, rate 180, narrow QRS, no visible P waves

**What rhythm is this?**

<details>
<summary>Click for answer</summary>

**Answer:** SVT (Supraventricular Tachycardia)

**Why:**
- Rate 150-250 (classic SVT range)
- Regular rhythm
- Narrow QRS (supraventricular origin)
- No visible P waves (hidden in QRS or T wave)

**Action:**
- Vagal maneuvers first (if stable)
  - Valsalva (bear down)
  - Carotid massage (if no bruits, < 65 years old)
- If no conversion:
  - Adenosine 6 mg rapid IV push (followed by 20 mL NS flush)
  - If no conversion: 12 mg
  - If no conversion: 12 mg again
- If unstable (chest pain, hypotension, altered mental status):
  - Synchronized cardioversion (50-100 J)

**Clinical Pearl:** Adenosine works in >90% of SVT cases. It's diagnostic and therapeutic.

</details>

### Case 3: The Irregular Rhythm

**Patient:** 76-year-old male, weakness, no chest pain  
**Vitals:** BP 118/74, HR "130-160", RR 18  
**ECG:** Irregularly irregular rhythm, no clear P waves, narrow QRS, undulating baseline

**What is this?**

<details>
<summary>Click for answer</summary>

**Answer:** Atrial Fibrillation with Rapid Ventricular Response

**Why:**
- Irregularly irregular (hallmark of afib)
- No P waves
- Undulating baseline (fibrillatory waves)
- Narrow QRS (conducted through AV node normally)
- Rate > 100 = RVR

**Action:**
- Check for hemodynamic instability
  - If stable: Rate control (diltiazem, metoprolol)
  - If unstable: Synchronized cardioversion (120-200 J)
- Assess stroke risk (anticoagulation needed if chronic)
- Look for reversible causes: sepsis, PE, hyperthyroidism, alcohol

**Clinical Pearl:** Afib with RVR in a sick patient often means something else is wrong (sepsis, PE, MI). Treat the cause.

</details>

### Case 4: The Wide Complex

**Patient:** 68-year-old male, chest pain, diaphoretic, altered  
**Vitals:** BP 82/50, HR 190, RR 26  
**ECG:** Regular rhythm, rate 190, wide QRS (0.16 sec), no clear P waves

**What's your move?**

<details>
<summary>Click for answer</summary>

**Answer:** Ventricular Tachycardia (unstable) â†’ immediate cardioversion

**Why:**
- Wide QRS (â‰¥0.12 sec)
- Rate > 100
- Regular rhythm
- Hemodynamically unstable (hypotensive, altered)

**Action:**
- **Immediate synchronized cardioversion** (don't delay)
  - 100 J biphasic (or 200 J monophasic)
  - Sedate if conscious (etomidate, versed, ketamine)
- IV access
- Amiodarone 150 mg over 10 min after conversion (if needed)
- Transport to PCI-capable center
- Continuous monitoring

**Clinical Pearl:** Wide complex tachycardia in an unstable patient = VT until proven otherwise. Don't waste time trying to differentiate. Cardiovert.

</details>

### Case 5: The Slow Heart

**Patient:** 82-year-old female, syncope, fell  
**Vitals:** BP 88/52, HR 38, RR 16  
**ECG:** Regular rhythm, rate 38, P waves present but no relationship to QRS, narrow QRS

**What's the rhythm?**

<details>
<summary>Click for answer</summary>

**Answer:** Third-Degree (Complete) AV Block

**Why:**
- Bradycardia
- P waves present
- **No relationship between P waves and QRS** (AV dissociation)
- Narrow QRS suggests junctional escape rhythm

**Action:**
- **Transcutaneous pacing** (patient is symptomatic)
  - Start at 60-70 bpm
  - Increase mA until capture
  - Sedate if conscious (painful)
- Atropine 0.5 mg IV (may not work in complete block, but try)
- Dopamine or epinephrine infusion if no pacer available
- IV fluids
- Transport for transvenous pacemaker

**Clinical Pearl:** Complete heart block = pacemaker needed (temporary or permanent). Medical management is a bridge, not definitive.

</details>
