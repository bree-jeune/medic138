## Explanation / Breakdown

### The Framework: ROME + Compensation Check

#### Step 1: Look at the pH
- **pH < 7.35** â†’ Acidosis (problem)

- If **pH and CO2 move in opposite directions** â†’ **Respiratory problem**

  - pH â†“ and HCO3 â†“ = Metabolic Acidosis

#### Step 3: Check for Compensation

- **Respiratory problems** â†’ kidneys compensate (takes 24-48 hours)
- **Metabolic problems** â†’ lungs compensate (takes minutes to hours)

**Compensation Rules:**
- **Partial compensation:** pH abnormal, but other value moving to help

### Real-World Translation

**Respiratory Acidosis (pH â†“, CO2 â†‘)**
- **Think:** Not breathing enough, retaining CO2
- **Causes:** COPD exacerbation, opioid overdose, neuromuscular weakness, airway obstruction
- **Fix:** Improve ventilation (BiPAP, intubation, reverse sedation)

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake #1: Calling It "Compensated" When pH Is Abnormal
If the pH is outside 7.35â€“7.45, it's NOT fully compensated. It's **partially compensated** or **uncompensated**.

**Example:**  
pH 7.28, CO2 68, HCO3 31  
This is **partially compensated respiratory acidosis**â€”not "compensated."

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Oxygenation
You can have perfect acid-base balance and still be hypoxic. Always check PaO2.

### Mistake #3: Forgetting Mixed Disorders
Sometimes patients have BOTH respiratory and metabolic problems. Look for:
- CO2 and HCO3 both moving in the wrong direction
- pH more abnormal than expected

### Mistake #4: Using ABGs to Diagnose Everything
ABGs show physiology, not etiology. They tell you what's happening, not always why.

### Mistake #5: Over-Trusting "Normal" pH
A pH of 7.38 can still represent serious underlying problems if both CO2 and HCO3 are abnormal (full compensation).

## Clinical Pearls

ðŸ’Ž **pH drives everything.** If pH is wrong, the patient is unstableâ€”even if they look okay right now.

ðŸ’Ž **CO2 is fast, kidneys are slow.** Respiratory compensation happens in minutes. Metabolic compensation takes days.

ðŸ’Ž **Chronic vs. Acute matters.** COPD patients live at CO2 60-65. That same number in an overdose is a disaster.

ðŸ’Ž **Don't chase numbers in chronic patients.** A COPD patient with pH 7.38, CO2 58, HCO3 32 is at their baseline. Don't hyperventilate them.

ðŸ’Ž **Lactate + low HCO3 = serious.** This is lactic acidosis until proven otherwise. Think sepsis, shock, bowel ischemia.

ðŸ’Ž **If pH is < 7.20, the patient is in trouble.** Period. Doesn't matter what the cause is.

ðŸ’Ž **Base Excess (BE) helps.** Negative BE = metabolic acidosis. Positive BE = metabolic alkalosis. Ignore it for respiratory problems.

ðŸ’Ž **Know your patient.** ABG interpretation without clinical context is worthless.
