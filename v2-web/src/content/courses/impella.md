## Explanation / Breakdown

### What is the Impella?

- The **Impella** is a percutaneous ventricular assist device that directly unloads the left ventricle, augments cardiac output, and supports end-organ perfusion while “buying time” for a failing heart.
- Consists of a microaxial flow pump inserted retrograde across the aortic valve into the LV.
- “P-series” includes **Impella CP, 2.5, 5.0, 5.5** — the number ≈ peak flow in L/min.


### Indications

- Cardiogenic shock (especially acute MI or post-cardiotomy)
- High-risk PCI “protected” cases
- Temporary bridge to transplant or surgical intervention


### How Does It Work?

- Sucks blood from LV → expels into ascending aorta
- Reduces LV pressure/volume (unloads LV), improves coronary perfusion, preserves end-organ flow
- Run at adjustable “P” (performance) levels (e.g., P4–P8)


### Key EMS Realities

- **Impella support is device-dependent; a failed pump can lead to rapid deterioration.**
- Moves are higher risk: *patient movement = dislodgment risk*, especially during load/unload
- The Impella console runs alarms for flow, placement, power, and purge system issues


#### Monitoring and Troubleshooting

- **Vitals**: MAP, CI, LVEDP, pulses
- **Console**: Flow (L/min), purge pressure, alarms
- **Waveform**: “Placement signal” — review with sending team if available
- **Physical assessment**: Limb perfusion at access site, blood return/colors, new murmurs


#### Transport Red Flags

- Sudden **loss of flow, low output alarms, rising pulmonary edema, or hemodynamic collapse** can mean device failure or migration
- Bleeding at access, hemolysis (pink urine), extremity ischemia: all require rapid intervention or escalation to sending/receiving MD

## Common Mistakes

- Not confirming secure device connections before movement.
- Relying solely on the monitor; not physically assessing pulses and perfusion.
- Ignoring low flow or purge alarms until profound instability.
- Transporting with device drips (e.g., heparin) unattached or misprogrammed.
- *Assuming staff at the next stop are more Impella-competent than you* — always check.

***

## Clinical Pearls

- Tape Impella lines well to avoid accidental dislodgment in transport.
- Know your critical numbers: *minimum mean arterial pressure, minimum device flow, purge pressure limits*.
- Always keep spare batteries and power cables accessible.
- Familiarize yourself with “emergency off” and hand-crank capabilities (if provided).
- If in doubt, use your patient’s MAP, pulses, mentation, and urine output as reality checks — not just the device readouts.

***

## Decision Trees

**Impella Management in Transport:**

1. Is the device secured and powered?
    - No → Secure lines, verify battery/power
    - Yes → Continue to monitoring
2. Are device parameters within accepted ranges? (flow, purge, MAP)
    - No → Troubleshoot per alarm (reposition, check lines, contact MD as needed)
    - Yes → Continue monitoring
3. Is patient perfusing? (pulses, LOC, U/O, color)
    - No → Suspect Impella failure/malposition; escalate immediately
    - Yes → Continue with current plan, repeat frequent checks

## Practice Cases

### Case 1: The Dislodgment

- Pt: 70M, anterior MI supported by Impella CP at P6, sedated.
- **10 min into air transport:** Alarms “Low flow,” MAP falls to 48.
- **Timeline:** 0 min—departure stable, 10 min—sudden alarm, 12 min—loss of distal pulses.
- Vitals: HR 120, BP 80/48, SpO₂ 92%, U/O minimal
> What are your next moves? What clues suggest the likely problem?


### Case 2: Purge Alarm

- Pt: 62F, postcardiotomy shock, purge pressure alarm mid-flight.
- **Timeline:** 0 min—stable on heparin purge, 8 min—alarm, 12 min—pink urine.
- Vitals: HR 96, BP 90/58, SpO₂ 94%, U/O normal
> What does a purge alarm mean? How do you troubleshoot in-flight?


### Case 3: Sudden Hemodynamic Crash

- Pt: 55M, AMI, on Impella 5.0, alert at start.
- Suddenly stands up during stretcher transfer; console alarms for placement.
- **Timeline:** 0 min—bed transfer, 2 min—sudden shock, 4 min—no palpable fem pulse.
- Vitals: HR 150, BP 60/30, SpO₂ 86%
> What happened physiologically, and how would you rapidly assess/correct?


### Case 4: Bleeding Complication

- Pt: 68F, Impella CP (fem access), arrives with expanding groin hematoma.
- **Timeline:** 0 min—handoff, 5 min—MAP drops from 70 to 58, progressing pallor.
- Vitals: HR 128, BP 82/58, SpO₂ 96%, U/O normal
> What’s likely, and what are your priorities during flight?


### Case 5: Power Failure

- Pt: 75M, Impella 2.5, device battery runs low during ambulance transfer.
- **Timeline:** 0 min—nearing destination, 4 min—power warning, 5 min—Impella stops, BP plummets.
- Vitals: HR 110, BP 60/40, SpO₂ 90%
> What backup options do you have, and how can you mitigate patient risk?


### Case 6: Device Weaning in Transit

- Pt: 58M, post-high-risk PCI, Impella on P4.
- **Timeline:** 0 min—map stable at 65, 10 min—weaning requested by consultant via radio.
- Vitals: HR 92, BP 94/65, SpO₂ 97%
> What are your steps for safe down-titration and monitoring?
